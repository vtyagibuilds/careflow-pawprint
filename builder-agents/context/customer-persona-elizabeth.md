# Customer Persona — Elizabeth

**Lifecycle Event:** First-time pet owner, new puppy
**MVP Status:** Primary persona — all MVP decisions are validated against Elizabeth

---

## Who She Is

Elizabeth is in her late 20s or early 30s. She lives alone or with a partner. She has always wanted a dog but this is the first time she's actually done it — first puppy, first pet of any kind. She researched the decision for months before committing.

She is not impulsive. She got a golden retriever because she read that they're good for first-time owners. She already has a name picked out. She's been thinking about this for a while.

She is educated and digitally comfortable. She shops online naturally. She reads articles when she wants to learn something. She is not a "pet person" yet — she doesn't have tribal loyalty to a pet brand or community. That identity is just forming.

---

## The Moment She Is In

She just brought the puppy home within the last few days. She placed her first order — puppy food — because she needed something immediately and found this retailer through search or a recommendation.

She contacted support because she was anxious about her delivery arriving on time. The puppy is home now. She needs the food now. The support interaction was transactional — she just wanted to know when it was coming.

But during the chat, she mentioned it: *"this is my first puppy, my first pet ever."*

She didn't say it because she wanted help. She said it because she was a little overwhelmed and it came out naturally. She didn't expect anything from it.

---

## What She Knows and Doesn't Know

**She knows:**
- She got a golden retriever
- She needs puppy food (that's why she ordered)
- Puppies need vet visits (she has one scheduled or is trying to schedule one)
- This is going to be a lot of work

**She doesn't know:**
- What the vaccination schedule looks like and when each shot is due
- When to transition from puppy food to adult food
- How to handle the first few nights (separation anxiety, crate training)
- What she actually needs to buy beyond the basics she already ordered
- What's normal puppy behavior vs. what should concern her
- That she doesn't know what she doesn't know — she thinks she's more prepared than she is

---

## What She Actually Needs Right Now

1. **Reassurance** — that she's not doing this wrong, that the chaos of the first week is normal
2. **Practical guidance** — concrete next steps, not general advice. What to do this week, not someday
3. **Vet guidance** — when to go, what to ask, what shots are coming
4. **Training basics** — house training, crate training, the first commands
5. **Product clarity** — what else does she need that she hasn't thought of yet? She doesn't want to over-buy, but she doesn't want to be caught without something important
6. **Breed-specific content** — she got a golden retriever specifically; content that speaks to that feels more relevant than generic puppy advice

---

## What Would Make Her Open the Email

- The subject line acknowledges that she's a first-time owner — not a discount, not a promotion
- It feels like it was written for her specific situation, not blasted to a list
- It promises something useful, not something the retailer wants her to see
- It arrives within 24-48 hours of her support chat — while she's still in the thick of week one

---

## What Would Make Her Delete It (or Feel Manipulated)

- It leads with a promotion or discount — feels like they used her personal moment to sell to her
- Generic puppy content that doesn't acknowledge she's a first-time owner
- Too long — she doesn't have time to read an essay; she has a puppy
- Content that assumes she already knows things she doesn't (e.g., references to "your next vet visit" before she's had her first)
- Anything that feels like it was triggered by an algorithm rather than written by someone who understood her situation

---

## The Emotional Register

Elizabeth is excited but anxious. She wants to feel capable, not overwhelmed. An email that makes her feel like she's behind or doing something wrong will land badly. An email that makes her feel like she's on the right track — and here are a few things that will help — will land well.

The tone should be: warm, knowledgeable, practical. Like advice from a friend who's been through this before. Not clinical. Not salesy. Not cutesy.

---

## What This Persona Is Not

Elizabeth is not a "dog person" yet. She does not have existing brand loyalty. She is not in a dog owner community or Facebook group. She is figuring this out on her own, online, in real time. That's exactly why this moment matters — she has no existing anchor. The retailer has a genuine opportunity to be that anchor.
